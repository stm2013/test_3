Introduction

The scanning tunneling microscope was invented by Gerd Binning and Heinrich Rohrer in 1981 in IBM, for which they received the Nobel Prize in physics for in 1986. The tool is a powerful microscopic technique capable of achieving sub-nanometer resolution of surfaces and is today routinely used by scientists to visualize and image surface topographies based on the local density of states concept [1]. By using a probe with an atomically sharp tip and an applied bias, a highly sensitive tunneling current can be conducted into the sample. Consequently, scanning the surface and measuring the changes in tunneling current results in an image representing the surfaces electronic environment [2]. This enables researchers to obtain a better understanding about their subject on a molecular level. One advantage over other characterization methods is its great versatility as it can operate in air, vacuum and liquids under non ambient conditions.

However, one major disadvantage is the use of highly specialized equipment that is fragile and expensive. The commercial prices of quality microscopes can range from $30 000 to $150 000 and are thus inaccessible to nonprofessional users such as scientists in development countries. Via the introduction of cheap tunnelling microscopes, the market would be extended to applicants with limited budgets such as scientific communities in development countries, undergraduate students in engineering and science educations as well as hobbyists. This project thus revolves around the design and implementation of an open source low cost scanning tunnelling microscope built from standard components, designing a standard operating procedure and if time permits develop a business concept for the product. Stakeholders include engineering and scientific communities in development countries, scientific education and research institutes as well as nano enthusiasts.
